Growth traps

Five keys to a story that survives the scroll

Your message is one drop in an ocean of data. Most of it gets skimmed and forgotten. Five things decide whether a story lands or dissolves into the noise.

By Deimar Gutiérrez · Virtual CFO

Dec 15, 2015 ·2 min read ·Updated Jul 17, 2026

Listen to this article

One drop. That's your message in the daily flood of email, ads, and notifications your audience already learned to ignore. Most of what you publish gets skimmed and forgotten inside a scroll. So the real question isn't what to say. It's how to make one drop land.

If people don't connect the dots you laid out, they'll draw their own. You probably won't like the picture they finish. Worse, the message dissolves into the noise and you never hear from them again. The same problem shows up in a crowded inbox, where the story starts before the reader opens the message.

"There is only one thing in life worse than being talked about, and that is not being talked about." — Oscar Wilde

You need stories built to cut through. Not every story does. Most brands broadcast; few connect. Here are the five things that separate the two.

1. Build it for one audience

Your story isn't for everyone. It can't be. It has to land in your audience's world, with their settings and their characters. Before you write a line, map who they are: what they do, what they buy already, what problem you solve, what moves them to a decision. A story aimed at everyone lands on no one.

2. Aim for an emotion, not a feature list

Once you know the audience, name the job. Is the story meant to build trust, drive a sale, or teach something? The job picks the emotion, and not every product sells on the same feeling. Watch how a movie trailer yanks you through several emotions in ninety seconds. That's deliberate. People act on feeling and justify with features afterward, so lead with the feeling.

3. More scene, fewer words

A great story written out makes a good book. If you want attention, show it. People skim text and stop on images. A picture sets a scene in a second that a paragraph needs a minute to build. Use visuals to open the scene, then let the words do the turn.

4. Keep it simple

Simple is the hard one. A story's job is to connect, not to unload data points, and statistics don't move anyone. Use everyday characters your audience recognizes. Give it a real shape: a beginning, a middle, a turn, an end. Show character through action and let the reader fill the gaps. When they finish the picture themselves, it sticks.

5. Earn the click in the first line

Don't save the best for last. The opening carries the same weight as the payoff. Miss the hook and they leave, and every insight you buried after it leaves with them. Open with the sharpest thing you have, then keep the promise.
