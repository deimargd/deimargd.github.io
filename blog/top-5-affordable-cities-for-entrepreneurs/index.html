The Top 5 Not So Known Cities to Start a Business on a Budget

You've got a business idea, a small team, and maybe $50K in seed capital. You're staring at a world map, wondering where to plant your flag without burning through your runway before you even launch.

By Deimar Gutiérrez · Virtual CFO

Apr 10, 2023 ·5 min read ·Updated Apr 10, 2023

Listen to this article

You’ve got a business idea, a small team, and maybe $50K in seed capital. You’re staring at a world map, wondering where to plant your flag without burning through your runway before you even launch. San Francisco or New York might flash across the screen, but their rent and payroll numbers often chew up a budget before you’ve hired your first developer.

The real question isn’t where everyone else goes. It’s where your capital stretches furthest, where you build instead of survive. Here are six cities where founders find that leverage.

1. Da Nang, Vietnam

Da Nang is a central Vietnamese city of over one million residents, sitting between Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City. It has become one of the country’s top startup destinations — a thriving early-stage ecosystem, skilled workers, and a strategic location for Southeast Asian expansion.

Vietnam ranks 59th on the World Bank’s Ease of Doing Business index, a meaningful climb from previous years. Foreign investors can access a 10% preferential corporate income tax rate for 15 years on eligible projects, full tax exemption for the first four years, and a 50% reduction for the following nine years in certain sectors. Add robust ICT infrastructure, high-speed internet, and import tax exemptions, and the math gets interesting fast.

Pros

  • Government incentives for foreign founders, including tax holidays and startup grants
  • Low cost of living relative to other major Vietnamese cities
  • Strategic location between Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City

Cons

  • Limited English proficiency among the local population
  • Smaller talent pool than regional hubs like Singapore or Bangkok
  • Less direct access to international capital markets

2. Chiang Mai, Thailand

Thailand ranks 21st on Ease of Doing Business — among the strongest in Southeast Asia. Chiang Mai itself has become a digital-nomad capital: Nomad List named it the best city in Southeast Asia for remote work in 2020, and Forbes listed it among the top ten rising startup centers in 2018. The government’s Thailand 4.0 initiative continues to push capital and policy support toward technology and innovation.

The corporate income tax rate sits at 20%, with sector-specific exemptions and reductions on top. Combine that with low cost of living, an established co-working scene, and a deep community of remote founders, and Chiang Mai becomes a credible base for a lean team.

Pros

  • Lower cost of living and operating expenses than Bangkok
  • Strong digital-nomad and entrepreneur community
  • Skilled labor available, particularly in software and creative tech

Cons

  • Less local capital and investor density than regional hubs
  • Smaller domestic market for B2C plays
  • Infrastructure outside the city center can be uneven

3. Cluj-Napoca, Romania

Cluj-Napoca sits in the heart of Transylvania and has quietly become one of Eastern Europe’s strongest tech cities. Romania ranks 45th on the World Bank’s 2022 Ease of Doing Business report, and in 2019 the Financial Times named Cluj-Napoca the third-best city in Eastern Europe for foreign direct investment strategy.

Romania’s corporate income tax rate is 16% — among the lowest in the EU — with additional exemptions for qualifying projects. The local engineering talent pool is deep, particularly for software and R&D, and the city’s location makes regional travel inside the EU straightforward.

Pros

  • Strong technical talent pool, especially in software and R&D
  • Tax incentives and subsidies for qualifying businesses
  • Low cost of living relative to Western Europe

Cons

  • Smaller capital and investment ecosystem than Berlin or Warsaw
  • Limited direct access to non-EU markets
  • Language barrier for non-Romanian speakers

4. Medellin, Colombia

Medellin has rebuilt itself into one of Latin America’s most interesting startup cities. The 2thinknow Innovation Cities Index 2021 named it the 5th most innovative city in the world, and the 2022 Global Cities Index ranked it 10th globally for doing business.

Colombia ranks 67th on Ease of Doing Business — workable rather than frictionless — but the local fundamentals are strong: affordable housing, a growing co-working ecosystem, and a steadily improving technical talent base. The city has become a magnet for digital nomads and Latin-America-focused startups.

Pros

  • Growing startup ecosystem with government-backed grants and tax breaks
  • Improving technical talent pool, particularly in software
  • Low cost of living relative to other major Latin American cities

Cons

  • Security concerns can deter some investors and founders
  • Limited access to large foreign markets
  • Local language barrier for non-Spanish speakers

5. Ljubljana, Slovenia

Ljubljana is small, compact, and easy to operate from. Slovenia ranks 40th on the World Bank’s 2022 Ease of Doing Business report, and the European Commission has flagged the country as a leader in innovation, with strong public support for R&D.

Newly established companies access a reduced corporate income tax rate of 19% for the first two years of operation, with additional credits for sectors like research and development. The city’s position between Western Europe and the Balkans gives a small team unusually broad market access for its size.

Pros

  • Government tax breaks and innovation subsidies for new companies
  • Highly educated workforce, particularly in tech and R&D
  • Strategic position between Western Europe and the Balkans

Cons

  • Limited local capital pool relative to Berlin or Vienna
  • Smaller domestic market
  • Language barrier for non-Slovenian speakers

Bonus — 6. Tel Aviv, Israel

Tel Aviv breaks the “low cost” rule but earns its place. Startup Genome ranks it the 6th-best startup ecosystem in the world, driven by a deep tech industry and one of the most highly educated workforces on the planet.

The country offers a reduced corporate income tax rate of 12% for qualifying businesses, plus credits and exemptions for R&D-heavy companies. Capital is abundant, infrastructure is mature, and the founder community is unusually dense for a city of its size.

Pros

  • World-class technical talent pool
  • Strong government support for startups, including tax breaks and grants
  • Deep capital and investor ecosystem

Cons

  • High living and operating costs relative to the other cities on this list
  • Regional political risk can affect access to neighboring markets
  • Security concerns may deter some founders and investors

The pattern across all six: tax policy that rewards new companies, a workforce that punches above its cost, and a founder community already on the ground. None of them solve the hard problem — building something people want — but each one stops the cost base from killing you while you try.

Pick the city that matches your wedge, not the one that sounds best at a dinner party.
